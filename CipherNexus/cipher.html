Caesar Cipher

Caesar Cipher
What Is It?

The Caesar cipher, named after Julius Caesar who used it in his private correspondence around 58 BC, is one of the simplest and earliest known encryption techniques. Each letter in the plaintext is shifted a fixed number of positions down the alphabet. Caesar typically used a shift of 3, making it one of history's first military cryptography tools.

How It Works

Step 1: Choose a shift value (1–25). Step 2: For each letter in the plaintext, shift it forward by the shift amount. Step 3: Wrap around from Z to A. Mathematically: En(x) = (x + n) mod 26.
Example: "HELLO" with shift 3 → "KHOOR". Decryption reverses the shift.

Playfair Cipher

Playfair Cipher
What Is It?

Invented by Charles Wheatstone in 1854 but promoted by Lord Playfair, the Playfair cipher encrypts pairs of letters (digraphs) instead of single characters. It was used by the British military in World War I and by the US Coast Guard during World War II. It was the first literal digraph substitution cipher in history.

How It Works

Step 1: Build a 5×5 matrix using a keyword (I/J share a cell). Step 2: Break plaintext into digraphs (insert 'X' between repeated letters; pad odd length with 'X'). Step 3: For each digraph: if same row, take letters to the right; if same column, take letters below; otherwise form a rectangle and swap corners. Example: Key "PLAYFAIR", plain "HELLO" → "HE LX LO" → matrix encryption produces ciphertext.

Hill Cipher

Hill Cipher
What Is It?

Invented by Lester S. Hill in 1929, the Hill cipher was the first polygraphic cipher that could operate on more than three symbols at once using linear algebra. It uses matrix multiplication to transform blocks of plaintext into ciphertext, representing a major mathematical advance in cryptography.

How It Works

Step 1: Convert letters to numbers (A=0...Z=25). Step 2: Group into pairs. Step 3: Multiply each pair by the 2×2 key matrix (mod 26). Mathematically: C = K × P mod 26. Decryption: P = K-1 × C mod 26, where K-1 is the modular inverse of the determinant times the adjugate.
Example key: [[3,3],[2,5]] with plaintext "HI" → ciphertext "OK".

Monoalphabetic Substitution Cipher

Monoalphabetic Substitution Cipher
What Is It?

Monoalphabetic substitution ciphers map each letter of the alphabet to a unique substitute letter, creating a one-to-one mapping. Used since ancient times (including the Atbash cipher from Hebrew scripture), these ciphers were once considered unbreakable until frequency analysis was developed by Arab scholars such as Al-Kindi in the 9th century.

How It Works

Step 1: Create a substitution alphabet (a shuffled permutation of A-Z). Step 2: Replace each plaintext letter with its corresponding substitute. Decryption: Reverse the mapping.
Example: If A→Q, B→W, C→E..., then "ABC" → "QWE". The key space is 26! (over 400 septillion possible keys).

Vigenère Cipher

Vigenère Cipher
What Is It?

First described by Giovan Battista Bellaso in 1553 and later misattributed to Blaise de Vigenère, this cipher uses a keyword to apply different Caesar shifts to each letter. It was known as "le chiffre indéchiffrable" (the unbreakable cipher) for over 300 years until Charles Babbage and Friedrich Kasiski cracked it in the 19th century.

How It Works

Step 1: Choose a keyword. Step 2: Repeat the keyword to match the plaintext length (keystream). Step 3: Shift each plaintext letter by the corresponding keyword letter's position (A=0...Z=25). Mathematically: Ci = (Pi + Ki) mod 26.
Example: Plaintext "HELLO", keyword "KEY" → keystream "KEYKE" → ciphertext "RIJVS".
